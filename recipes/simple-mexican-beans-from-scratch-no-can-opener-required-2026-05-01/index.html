Simple Mexican Beans from Scratch (No Can Opener Required)

Published May 1, 2026 · Improv Oven

This simple Mexican beans recipe from scratch is the kind of thing that'll make you wonder why you ever bought the canned stuff. We're talking creamy, smoky, deeply flavored pinto beans cooked low and slow with aromatics until they're falling-apart tender and swimming in that gorgeous, thick broth called caldo. Once you nail this, it becomes your base for everything — tacos, rice bowls, tostadas, or just a bowl with warm tortillas on a Tuesday night.

Prep
10 mins
Cook
1 hour 45 mins
Total
1 hour 55 mins
Serves
6

Ingredients

  • 1 lb dried pinto beans, picked over and rinsed
  • 10 cups water, plus more if needed
  • 1/2 white onion, left in one chunk
  • 6 garlic cloves, smashed
  • 2 dried bay leaves
  • 1 dried ancho or guajillo chile, whole (optional but highly recommended)
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1.5 tsp kosher salt, plus more to taste
  • 2 tbsp lard or neutral oil
  • Small handful of fresh cilantro (stems are fine)

Instructions

  1. 1No soaking required, but if you have time, cover the beans with cold water and soak them overnight — they'll cook faster and be even creamier. If not, no stress, just rinse them well and move on.
  2. 2Add the rinsed beans to a large heavy pot — a Dutch oven is ideal. Pour in 10 cups of cold water. Drop in the onion chunk, smashed garlic cloves, bay leaves, the whole dried chile if you're using it, cumin, and the cilantro. Do NOT add the salt yet — adding it too early can make the skins tough.
  3. 3Bring everything to a boil over high heat, then immediately reduce to a low, steady simmer. You want small bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil. Skim off any foam that rises to the top in the first 10 minutes — that's just proteins releasing and it's totally normal.
  4. 4Cover the pot partially with a lid and let the beans cook for about 1 hour to 1 hour and 15 minutes, stirring occasionally and checking the water level. If the beans start peeking above the liquid, add hot water about a cup at a time to keep them submerged.
  5. 5Start testing the beans around the 1-hour mark. Grab a few and squish them between your fingers — they should be completely tender with no chalky center. If they're not there yet, keep going in 15-minute increments. Once they're soft, NOW add your salt and stir it in. Taste the broth and adjust.
  6. 6Fish out the bay leaves, the onion chunk, and the dried chile if you added one. Stir in the lard or oil — this gives the beans that silky, rich finish you get at good taquerias. Let everything simmer together uncovered for another 10 to 15 minutes so the broth thickens slightly and all those flavors get cozy.
  7. 7Taste one more time for salt. Serve the beans in deep bowls with plenty of that broth — the caldo is the best part. Hit them with fresh cilantro on top, maybe a squeeze of lime, and get some warm tortillas going on the side.
💡 Improv Tip

The secret weapon here is that dried whole chile — ancho gives you deep, chocolatey warmth while guajillo adds a brighter, slightly fruity kick. Neither one makes the beans spicy, just incredibly complex. You can find them for almost nothing in the Latin aisle or at any Mexican grocery store. Tear one open, shake out the seeds, and toss the whole thing right in the pot. Your kitchen is going to smell absolutely unreal.

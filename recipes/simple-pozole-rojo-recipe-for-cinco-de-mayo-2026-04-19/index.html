Simple Pozole Rojo Recipe for Cinco de Mayo

Published April 19, 2026 · Improv Oven

This simple pozole rojo recipe is your Cinco de Mayo secret weapon — a rich, brick-red broth loaded with tender pork and hominy that tastes like it simmered all day but comes together on a weeknight. We're using pantry staples and a few dried chiles to build that deep, smoky flavor without any fuss. Top it with all the fixings and watch the table go quiet in the best way possible.

Prep
20 mins
Cook
1 hour 10 mins
Total
1 hour 30 mins
Serves
6

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs boneless pork shoulder, cut into 2-inch chunks
  • 2 (15 oz) cans hominy, drained and rinsed
  • 4 dried guajillo chiles, stems and seeds removed
  • 2 dried ancho chiles, stems and seeds removed
  • 1 (14 oz) can diced tomatoes
  • 6 cups chicken broth
  • 1 medium white onion, half roughly chopped and half thinly sliced for garnish
  • 6 garlic cloves, peeled
  • 1 tsp dried oregano (Mexican oregano if you can find it)
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp salt, plus more to taste
  • 1/2 tsp black pepper
  • 2 tbsp neutral oil
  • For serving: shredded cabbage, sliced radishes, diced white onion, dried oregano, lime wedges, tostadas

Instructions

  1. 1Toast your dried guajillo and ancho chiles in a dry skillet over medium heat for about 30 to 45 seconds per side — just until they puff up slightly and smell amazing. Don't let them burn or your broth will turn bitter. Transfer them to a bowl and pour 2 cups of boiling water over them. Let them soak for 15 minutes until soft.
  2. 2While the chiles soak, season your pork chunks generously with salt and pepper. Heat the oil in a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Working in batches, sear the pork on all sides until you get a deep golden-brown crust, about 8 to 10 minutes total. Remove the pork and set aside — that sear is building your flavor base so don't skip it.
  3. 3In that same pot, add the roughly chopped onion and garlic cloves. Cook for 3 minutes until softened and picking up a little color. Pour in about a cup of the chicken broth to deglaze, scraping up all those beautiful brown bits stuck to the bottom.
  4. 4Drain the soaked chiles and add them to a blender along with the canned tomatoes, the cooked onion and garlic from the pot, cumin, oregano, and 1 cup of chicken broth. Blend on high until completely smooth, about 60 seconds. If your blender runs hot, let things cool slightly first and hold that lid down tight.
  5. 5Pour the chile sauce through a fine mesh strainer back into the pot, pressing with a spoon to get every bit of flavor through. Discard the solids. Turn the heat to medium and cook the sauce for 5 minutes, stirring frequently — it will darken and thicken slightly. This step wakes everything up.
  6. 6Add the seared pork back to the pot along with the remaining chicken broth. Bring everything to a boil, then reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 45 minutes until the pork is very tender and starting to pull apart. Taste and adjust salt as needed.
  7. 7Add the drained hominy to the pot and simmer uncovered for another 15 minutes so the hominy soaks up all that red broth goodness. Give it one final taste for salt and seasoning.
  8. 8Ladle the pozole into deep bowls and set out all your toppings — shredded cabbage, sliced radishes, fresh diced onion, dried oregano, and plenty of lime wedges. Tostadas on the side are non-negotiable. Let everyone build their own bowl and watch the magic happen.
💡 Improv Tip

The toppings are not decoration — they are part of the dish. Pozole without a good squeeze of lime and that crunch of raw cabbage is like playing music with no bass. Set everything out before the pot even hits the table so people can go to town. Also, this tastes even better the next day once that chile broth has had time to really settle into the hominy, so make a big batch and plan for leftovers.

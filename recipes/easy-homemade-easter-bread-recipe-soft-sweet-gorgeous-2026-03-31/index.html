Easy Homemade Easter Bread Recipe (Soft, Sweet & Gorgeous)

Published March 31, 2026 · Improv Oven

This easy homemade Easter bread recipe is the one your whole family is going to ask about every single year — promise. It's soft, slightly sweet, braided into that classic wreath shape with colorful dyed eggs nestled right in, and honestly it looks way more impressive than the effort it takes. Think of it like the bread version of a centerpiece that you can actually eat.

Prep
30 mins
Cook
30 mins
Total
3 hrs (includes rise time)
Serves
8-10 slices

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, plus a little extra for kneading
  • 2 1/4 teaspoons active dry yeast (1 standard packet)
  • 1/2 cup warm milk (not hot — think bath water warm)
  • 1/4 cup warm water
  • 1/4 cup granulated sugar
  • 3 tablespoons unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon anise seed or anise extract (optional but traditional)
  • Zest of 1 lemon or orange (optional — adds a beautiful brightness)
  • 4-6 raw eggs, dyed in your favorite Easter colors (they bake right in)
  • 1 egg beaten with 1 tablespoon water for egg wash
  • Colored sprinkles or pearl sugar for topping

Instructions

  1. 1Wake up your yeast first: In a small bowl, combine the warm water, 1 teaspoon of your sugar, and the yeast. Give it a quick stir and let it sit for about 5-10 minutes. You want to see it get foamy and bubbly — that means it's alive and ready to work. If nothing happens after 10 minutes, your water was probably too hot or too cold, or your yeast is old. Grab a new packet and try again — no shame, we've all been there.
  2. 2Make the dough: In a large bowl, whisk together the flour, remaining sugar, and salt. In a separate bowl or measuring cup, mix together the warm milk, softened butter, egg, vanilla extract, and anise or citrus zest if you're using them. Pour the wet ingredients plus your activated yeast mixture into the flour bowl and stir it all together with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms.
  3. 3Knead it out: Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface and knead it for about 8-10 minutes until it's smooth, elastic, and just barely tacky — not sticky. If it keeps sticking to your hands, dust in a tiny bit more flour, a tablespoon at a time. You want the dough to feel like a soft earlobe when it's ready (that's the real test, look it up).
  4. 4First rise: Shape the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, cover it with a clean kitchen towel or plastic wrap, and let it rise in a warm spot for about 1 to 1.5 hours until it's doubled in size. Miami homes are warm, so this usually goes fast — if your kitchen is cold, preheat your oven to 200°F, turn it off, and put the covered bowl inside.
  5. 5Shape the braid: Once risen, punch the dough down gently and divide it into 3 equal pieces. Roll each piece into a rope about 18-20 inches long. Pinch the three ropes together at one end and braid them loosely — not too tight, because the dough needs room to puff up again. Connect the ends into a circle (wreath shape) and place it on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Tuck your dyed raw eggs gently into the gaps of the braid, nestling them in snugly.
  6. 6Second rise: Cover the shaped loaf loosely with a towel and let it rise again for another 30-45 minutes. This second rise is what gives the bread that light, pillowy texture. Don't skip it.
  7. 7Bake it: Preheat your oven to 350°F. Brush the entire loaf gently with the egg wash — be careful around the eggs so you don't smear the dye. Scatter colored sprinkles or pearl sugar over the top. Bake for 25-30 minutes until the bread is deeply golden brown. If the top starts browning too fast, loosely tent it with foil for the last 10 minutes.
  8. 8Cool before cutting: Let the bread cool on a wire rack for at least 15-20 minutes before slicing. The eggs will have cooked fully in the oven and are totally safe to eat. Cut between the eggs to serve — everyone gets a slice and an egg. That's the move.
💡 Improv Tip

The dyed eggs go in raw — yes, raw — and they cook right inside the bread as it bakes. If you only have plain white eggs and no dye, hard boil them first and wrap them in foil so they hold their shape in the braid, then unwrap before baking to get color on them. Either way it works. Also, day-old slices make incredible French toast the next morning — that's not an accident, that's the plan.

Easy Molletes Recipe: Mexican Open-Face Beans and Cheese Dinner

Published May 3, 2026 · Improv Oven

This easy molletes recipe is your new secret weapon for those nights when you need something real on the table without spending an hour in the kitchen — we're talking Mexican open-face beans melted onto crusty bread with gooey cheese and fresh pico, done in 30 minutes flat. Molletes are basically Mexico's answer to the loaded toast, and once you make them for dinner you're going to wonder why you ever ordered takeout on a Tuesday. Budget-friendly, deeply satisfying, and honestly impressive for how little effort they ask of you.

Prep
10 mins
Cook
20 mins
Total
30 mins
Serves
4

Ingredients

  • 4 bolillo rolls or 1 large French baguette cut into 4 portions
  • 1 can (15 oz) pinto beans or black beans, drained
  • 2 tablespoons butter or olive oil
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1/2 teaspoon cumin
  • 1/2 teaspoon chili powder
  • Salt to taste
  • 2 cups shredded Oaxaca cheese or mozzarella
  • 1/2 cup crumbled cotija cheese (optional but worth it)
  • 2 roma tomatoes, finely diced
  • 1/4 white onion, finely diced
  • 1 jalapeño, finely diced (seeds removed if you want it mild)
  • Juice of 1 lime
  • Small handful of fresh cilantro, chopped

Instructions

  1. 1Preheat your oven to 400°F and line a baking sheet with foil. Slice your bolillos or baguette portions in half lengthwise so you have open-face pieces, then arrange them cut-side up on the baking sheet. Slide them in for about 5 minutes just to start getting a little dry and toasty — this is important so the bread doesn't go soggy under those beans.
  2. 2While the bread toasts, make your quick pico. Combine the diced tomatoes, white onion, jalapeño, cilantro, lime juice, and a good pinch of salt in a small bowl. Toss it all together and set it aside — even 10 minutes of sitting makes those flavors come together beautifully.
  3. 3Heat the butter or olive oil in a medium skillet over medium heat. Add the minced garlic and let it sizzle for about 60 seconds until fragrant. Add your drained beans right into the pan along with the cumin, chili powder, and a generous pinch of salt. Use the back of a wooden spoon or a fork to smash roughly half the beans — you want a chunky, spreadable texture, not a smooth purée. Cook everything together for 3 to 4 minutes until the beans are heated through and slightly thickened.
  4. 4Pull the bread out of the oven and spread a generous layer of the smashed beans all the way to the edges of each piece — don't be shy here, this is the soul of the dish. Cover each piece heavily with shredded Oaxaca cheese or mozzarella so the beans are basically blanketed.
  5. 5Return the loaded bread to the oven for 8 to 10 minutes until the cheese is fully melted and starting to bubble. If you want a little color on top, switch the broiler on for the last 2 minutes and watch it closely — you want golden and melty, not burnt.
  6. 6Pull them out and immediately top each mollete with a big spoonful of your fresh pico and a sprinkle of cotija if you have it. Serve right away while the cheese is still stretchy and the bread is still crisp on the bottom. A few dashes of hot sauce over the top and you are absolutely eating good tonight.
💡 Improv Tip

The beans are where all the flavor lives, so taste them before they go on the bread — if they feel flat, a little more salt and a tiny splash of the bean liquid from the can will bring them right to life. Also, bolillos are the traditional roll and they are genuinely worth tracking down at a Latin bakery or the bread section of any Latin grocery store — that slight crunch and chew makes a real difference over a regular hot dog bun situation.

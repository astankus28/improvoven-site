Easy Homemade Teriyaki Sauce Recipe (Better Than the Bottle)

Published May 3, 2026 · Improv Oven

This easy homemade teriyaki sauce recipe is about to change how you cook on a Tuesday night — we're talking four pantry staples, one small saucepan, and about ten minutes of your time. No weird preservatives, no mystery ingredients, just that glossy, sweet-savory goodness you can drizzle on everything from chicken thighs to roasted veggies. Once you make it yourself, that bottle in the store aisle is going to look real unnecessary.

Prep
5 mins
Cook
10 mins
Total
15 mins
Serves
6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup soy sauce (low-sodium works great)
  • 1/4 cup water
  • 2 tablespoons honey (or brown sugar if that's what you've got)
  • 1 tablespoon rice vinegar (apple cider vinegar works in a pinch)
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon fresh ginger, grated (or 1/4 teaspoon ground ginger)
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch
  • 2 tablespoons cold water (for the cornstarch slurry)

Instructions

  1. 1In a small saucepan over medium heat, combine the soy sauce, 1/4 cup water, honey, rice vinegar, garlic, and ginger. Stir everything together and let it come to a gentle simmer — you'll start smelling amazing things almost immediately.
  2. 2While that's warming up, mix your cornstarch with the 2 tablespoons of cold water in a small bowl or cup until smooth. This is your slurry — it's what gives the sauce that beautiful glossy thickness instead of watery sadness.
  3. 3Once the soy sauce mixture is simmering, pour in the cornstarch slurry while stirring constantly. Keep stirring for about 1 to 2 minutes as the sauce thickens up and gets that gorgeous lacquered look.
  4. 4Taste it. Seriously, taste it right now. Want it sweeter? Add a little more honey. More savory? A splash more soy sauce. That's the whole point of making it yourself — you're the boss here.
  5. 5Remove from heat and let it cool slightly before using. It will continue to thicken as it cools, so don't panic if it seems a little thin at first. Use it immediately as a marinade, glaze, or dipping sauce — or store it in a jar in the fridge for up to two weeks.
💡 Improv Tip

If your sauce gets too thick after refrigerating, just whisk in a teaspoon of warm water at a time until it loosens back up — it comes right back to life. And listen, this sauce is the cure for a boring protein situation: brush it on chicken thighs, toss it with stir-fried vegetables, dunk some dumplings in it, whatever you need. One batch goes a long way.
